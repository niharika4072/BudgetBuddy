Banking Basics

Everything you need to know about bank accounts, interest, and everyday banking — explained simply.

💳
Must Know
Savings Account vs Current Account

Savings Account is for everyday people — you park your money here and earn interest (usually 2.5%–7% per year). Current Account is for businesses — unlimited transactions but no interest earned.

💡 Example

Priya is a college student. She opens a Savings Account with SBI and deposits her ₹5,000 pocket money. She earns ₹125–₹350 interest per year just for keeping money there. A shopkeeper opens a Current Account to handle hundreds of daily transactions.

✅ As a student, always open a zero-balance savings account — SBI Basic Savings, Kotak 811, or HDFC Basic are great options. No minimum balance required!
BeginnerBanking
📊
Core Concept
Simple Interest vs Compound Interest

Simple Interest (SI) = you earn interest only on the original amount. Compound Interest (CI) = you earn interest on the original amount PLUS the interest you already earned. CI is more powerful!

💡 Example

Rahul invests ₹10,000 at 10% for 3 years.
SI = ₹10,000 × 10% × 3 = ₹3,000 interest
CI = ₹10,000 → ₹11,000 → ₹12,100 → ₹13,310 (₹3,310 interest)
Compound interest gave him ₹310 more!

✅ Always look for accounts and investments that offer compound interest. The longer you stay invested, the more powerful compounding becomes — this is called the "8th wonder of the world"!
BeginnerConcept

Banking
Fixed Deposit (FD)

A Fixed Deposit is like locking your money in a bank safe for a fixed period (7 days to 10 years) and getting a guaranteed interest rate. The longer you lock, the higher the rate. FDs are 100% safe since deposits up to ₹5 lakh are insured by DICGC.

💡 Example

Arjun has ₹20,000 saved after internship. He puts it in a 1-year FD at 7.1% (SBI rate). After 1 year he gets back ₹20,000 + ₹1,420 = ₹21,420. Zero risk, guaranteed returns!

✅ Use FD for money you won't need for 6–12 months. Senior citizens get 0.5% extra interest. Best rates currently: Small Finance Banks (8%–9%).
⚠️ Breaking an FD early usually costs 0.5%–1% penalty. Only invest money you won't need urgently.
SafeBanking

Banking
Recurring Deposit (RD)

An RD lets you deposit a fixed small amount every month instead of one large lump sum. Great for students who want to save regularly. Interest rates are similar to FD.

💡 Example

Sneha deposits ₹500 every month in an RD for 12 months at 6.5%. After 1 year she has deposited ₹6,000 but gets back ₹6,230 — she earned ₹230 just by saving a small amount each month!

✅ RD is the perfect "first investment" for students. Start with as little as ₹100/month. It builds the saving habit AND earns you money.

Must Know
CIBIL Score / Credit Score

Your CIBIL score (300–900) is your financial "report card." Banks check this before giving you any loan or credit card. A score above 750 means you're trusted; below 600 means banks may reject your loan.

💡 Example

Vikram needs a ₹2 lakh education loan. His CIBIL score is 780 (Excellent) — the bank approves it at 9% interest. His friend Rohit has a score of 580 because he missed credit card payments — the bank rejects his loan application.

✅ Build your CIBIL score early: get a small credit card, use it for minor purchases, and ALWAYS pay the full bill on time. Check your free score on CIBIL.com or Paytm once a year.
⚠️ Never miss an EMI or credit card due date. One missed payment can drop your score by 50–100 points!

Investments — Make Your Money Work

The earlier you start investing, the wealthier you become. Here's every investment option explained simply.

🧑‍🏫 Mentor's Golden Advice

Investing is not gambling. It's putting your money to work so that it grows while you sleep. The biggest mistake young Indians make is waiting to invest "when I have more money." Start with ₹500/month today. In 20 years, at 12% returns, ₹500/month becomes ₹4.99 lakh. Start at 30 instead of 20, and it becomes only ₹1.98 lakh. Time is your biggest asset.

Most Popular
SIP — Systematic Investment Plan

SIP means investing a fixed amount every month into a mutual fund automatically. You don't need to time the market. When markets are down, your ₹500 buys more units. When markets are up, your units are worth more. This is called Rupee Cost Averaging.

💡 Example

Pooja starts a ₹1,000/month SIP in a Nifty 50 index fund at age 22. She invests for 10 years (total ₹1.2 lakh invested). At 12% average return, she has ₹2.32 lakh at age 32 — nearly double her investment!

✅ Best apps to start SIP: Groww, Zerodha Coin, Paytm Money — all free, zero commission. Start with Nifty 50 index fund if you're a beginner. No knowledge needed!
Beginner FriendlyHigh Returns

Government Backed
PPF — Public Provident Fund

PPF is a government-guaranteed savings scheme with 15-year lock-in. The interest is completely tax-free. You can invest ₹500 to ₹1.5 lakh per year. This is one of the safest long-term investments in India.

💡 Example

Amit invests ₹1,000/month in PPF for 15 years = ₹1.8 lakh invested. At 7.1% compounded, he gets back ₹3.25 lakh tax-free. He also saves ₹3,120 per year in income tax under Section 80C!

✅ Open PPF at any post office, SBI, or online via net banking. Best for: building a long-term corpus for marriage, education, or retirement. EEE tax status — Exempt at all 3 stages!
Tax FreeGovernment Backed

For Salaried
EPF — Employee Provident Fund

When you start a salaried job, 12% of your basic salary is automatically deducted for EPF. Your employer also contributes 12%. This is a retirement fund that earns ~8.25% interest. You can't withdraw easily, but that's the point — it forces you to save!

💡 Example

Neha joins a company with ₹25,000/month basic salary. Every month ₹3,000 is deducted from her salary + her company adds ₹3,000 = ₹6,000 saved every month automatically towards her retirement. After 5 years: ~₹4.5 lakh saved!

✅ Never withdraw EPF when switching jobs — transfer it using UAN (Universal Account Number). Withdrawing early means losing your employer's contribution and paying tax!

Stocks — Direct Equity Investing

Buying a stock means buying a tiny piece of a company. If the company grows, your stock value goes up. If it does badly, it goes down. Stocks can give very high returns but are volatile (go up and down a lot).

💡 Example

Ravi bought 10 shares of Infosys at ₹1,200 each in 2020 (₹12,000 total). By 2023, the price was ₹1,500 — his investment became ₹15,000 (25% profit) PLUS he got ₹600 in dividends. Total gain: ₹3,600!

⚠️ Never invest in stocks money you need in the next 1–2 years. Study a company's fundamentals (P/E ratio, revenue growth, debt) before buying. Never invest on tips or WhatsApp forwards!
✅ Open a Demat account with Zerodha, Groww, or Upstox. Start small — buy 1 share of a company you believe in and learn from experience.

Long Term
NPS — National Pension System

NPS is a government pension scheme where you invest regularly and get a pension after age 60. It invests in both equity and debt. You get extra tax benefit of ₹50,000 under Section 80CCD(1B) — over and above the ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit!

💡 Example

Deepak, 25, invests ₹2,000/month in NPS for 35 years until age 60. At ~10% average return, he accumulates ~₹76 lakh. He gets ₹30 lakh as lump sum + monthly pension from the remaining ₹46 lakh. Never run out of money in retirement!

✅ Open NPS online at eNPS.nsdl.com in 10 minutes. Even ₹1,000/month contribution from age 22 creates a comfortable retirement corpus.

The Indian government has hundreds of schemes designed to help you. Most people never claim these benefits. Know your rights!

Banking Scheme

Under Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana, any Indian citizen can open a zero-balance bank account and get: free RuPay debit card, ₹2 lakh accident insurance, ₹30,000 life insurance (Jeevan Jyoti), overdraft facility of ₹10,000 after 6 months of good usage.

💡 Example

Sunita from a village opens a Jan Dhan account at her nearest bank with just her Aadhaar card. She gets a free RuPay card. If she meets with an accident, her family gets ₹2 lakh — completely free. She also receives her LPG subsidy, MNREGA wages directly in this account.

✅ How to apply: Visit any bank branch with Aadhaar + PAN (or just Aadhaar if no PAN). Completely free. Available at all government and private banks.

A government pension scheme for the unorganised sector (shopkeepers, drivers, farmers, freelancers). You contribute a small amount monthly, and after age 60, you get a guaranteed pension of ₹1,000–₹5,000/month for life. Government also contributes.

Insurance — Protect What You've Built

Insurance is not an investment — it's protection. Understand what you actually need and avoid being oversold policies you don't need.

🧑‍🏫 The Mentor's Truth About Insurance

Most agents will try to sell you "insurance + investment" plans (endowment, money-back, ULIPs). The truth: they give poor returns AND poor coverage. The smart approach: Buy TERM insurance for protection. Invest separately in mutual funds for returns. This is called "Buy Term + Invest the Rest."

🏥
Most Important
Health Insurance — Must Have!

Health insurance pays your hospital bills if you fall sick or meet with an accident. One serious illness can wipe out years of savings. For a 25-year-old, a ₹5 lakh health insurance costs only ₹4,000–₹8,000/year!

💡 Example

Rohit, 24, has no health insurance. He gets dengue with complications — hospital bill: ₹1.8 lakh. He had to borrow from family. His colleague Priya has a ₹5 lakh health policy for ₹5,500/year. Same hospital, same bill — she paid ₹0 from her own pocket.

✅ Get a ₹5–10 lakh floater health plan covering your whole family. Compare on PolicyBazaar. Check for: room rent limit (prefer "no capping"), list of network hospitals, pre-existing disease waiting period (choose shortest).
Highest PriorityFamily
👨‍👩‍👧
Life Insurance
Term Insurance — Pure Life Cover

Term insurance pays a large sum to your family if YOU die. No maturity benefit if you survive (that's the point — it's pure protection, not savings). For ₹1 crore cover, a healthy 25-year-old pays only ₹7,000–₹10,000/year.

💡 Example

Anand, 26, sole earner, buys ₹1 crore term policy for ₹8,500/year. He sadly dies in an accident at 35. His wife and children receive ₹1 crore — enough to pay off the home loan, fund the children's education, and invest for monthly income. His family's life doesn't fall apart.

✅ Buy term insurance if someone depends on your income (parents, spouse, children). Buy from LIC, HDFC Life, ICICI Prudential, or Max Life — check claim settlement ratio (should be above 98%).
⚠️ Do NOT buy endowment/money-back LIC policies as investments. They give only 4–5% returns. Invest in mutual funds instead.

Tax Saving & Filing ITR

Save thousands in tax legally. Filing ITR is not just a duty — it helps you get loans, visas, and refunds!

💰
Tax Saving
Section 80C — Save ₹46,800 Tax Per Year

Under Section 80C, you can claim deduction of up to ₹1.5 lakh from your taxable income by investing in specified instruments. For someone in the 30% tax bracket, this saves ₹46,800 in tax annually!

💡 What Qualifies Under 80C?

PPF, ELSS Mutual Funds, LIC Premium, EPF, NSC, 5-year FD, Sukanya Samriddhi, Post Office TD (5yr), Tuition fees for children, Home loan principal repayment.

✅ Best 80C choice for young investors: ELSS Mutual Funds — only 3-year lock-in (shortest among all 80C options) AND highest potential returns (12%–15%). Use Groww or Zerodha to invest.
Tax Saving₹1.5 Lakh Limit
🏥
Tax Saving
Section 80D — Save Tax on Health Insurance

Premiums paid for health insurance are tax-deductible under 80D. Up to ₹25,000 for self/family + ₹25,000 for parents (₹50,000 if parents are senior citizens). Total possible deduction: ₹75,000!

💡 Example

Ravi pays ₹12,000/year for his family's health insurance + ₹18,000 for his parents (senior citizens). He claims ₹12,000 + ₹18,000 = ₹30,000 deduction under 80D. If he's in 20% tax bracket, he saves ₹6,000 in tax — the insurance is basically discounted by 50%!

✅ Moral: Getting health insurance is not just protection, it also saves you tax. It's a win-win!
Tax SavingHealth

Must Do
Why File ITR Even If You Have No Tax to Pay?

Many students and freshers think "I don't earn enough to pay tax, so I don't need to file ITR." That's WRONG. Filing ITR is beneficial even at zero tax.

  • Get TDS refund (if bank deducted tax on FD interest)
  • Required for visa applications (US, UK, Canada, etc.)
  • Acts as income proof for home loans, car loans
  • Carry forward capital losses to offset future gains
  • Shows you're a responsible citizen — builds financial record
✅ File ITR free at incometax.gov.in before July 31 every year. Use ITR-1 form for salaried with income under ₹50 lakh. Takes 20–30 minutes.
All CitizensFree to File

Golden Rules of Personal Finance

10 rules that will change your financial life forever. Memorise these.

1

Pay Yourself First

The moment your salary arrives, immediately transfer your savings amount to a separate account or investment. Spend what's left — never save what's left after spending. If you save ₹3,000 first, you WILL adjust to the rest. If you save what's left, there's usually nothing left.

2

The 50-30-20 Rule

Spend 50% of take-home income on NEEDS (rent, food, transport), 30% on WANTS (entertainment, dining out, shopping), 20% on SAVINGS and investments. This simple rule prevents overspending and ensures you always make progress.

3

Emergency Fund Before Investing

Before putting money in stocks or mutual funds, build 3–6 months of expenses as an emergency fund in a liquid account (savings account or liquid mutual fund). Without this, one medical emergency can force you to sell investments at a loss.

4

Avoid Debt for Depreciating Assets

Never take a loan for something that loses value — phone, TV, clothes, bike (unless for income). Good debt = education loan, home loan (assets that grow or earn). Bad debt = credit card EMI for gadgets, personal loans for vacations.

5

Start Investing at 21, Not 31

₹1,000/month from age 21 to 60 at 12% = ₹1.17 crore. ₹1,000/month from age 31 to 60 at 12% = ₹35 lakh. Starting 10 years late costs you ₹82 lakh. Compounding needs TIME more than it needs money.

6

Never Invest in What You Don't Understand

If someone promises "guaranteed 30% monthly returns" — run. If you don't understand how a scheme makes money, don't put money in it. Stick to RDs, PPF, index funds, and FDs when starting out. Complexity ≠ better returns.

7

Insure Before You Invest

Get health insurance and term insurance BEFORE you invest in stocks or mutual funds. One hospital bill without insurance can destroy years of investments. Insurance is the foundation of all financial planning.

8

Track Every Rupee (That's What BudgetBuddy is For!)

You cannot manage what you don't measure. Track all expenses for 90 days. You'll be shocked at how much goes on food delivery, impulse online shopping, and subscriptions you forgot about. Awareness is the first step to control.

9

Increase Your Income, Not Just Cut Expenses

Frugality has limits — you can only cut so much. But income can grow unlimited. Invest in skills (courses, certifications), build a side income (freelancing, content creation, tutoring). A ₹5,000/month side income invested from age 22 = ₹5.8 crore by retirement.

10

Review Your Finances Every Month

Set a "money date" with yourself on the last day of every month. Review: Did I stick to budget? Is my SIP running? Are my insurance premiums paid? Any unused subscriptions? Monthly reviews prevent small leaks from becoming financial floods.

Quick Questions Answered
🤔 How much should I save as a college student with ₹3,000/month pocket money?▼

Even saving ₹300–₹500/month is excellent at this stage. Start a Post Office RD or a small SIP. The amount matters less than the habit. Someone who saves ₹500/month consistently from age 20 will be wealthier at 40 than someone who starts saving ₹10,000/month at 30.

💳 Should I get a credit card as a student?▼

Yes — but only if you're disciplined. A credit card used well builds your CIBIL score, gives cashback/rewards, and provides 45-day interest-free credit. The golden rule: ALWAYS pay 100% of the bill before the due date. Never pay minimum due. Set up auto-pay for the full amount. Never treat a credit card as extra money.

📊 Mutual fund vs FD — which is better?▼

For short term (under 3 years) and risk-averse people: FD wins — guaranteed returns, no stress. For long term (5+ years): Mutual funds (especially index funds) historically beat FD returns significantly (12% vs 7%). The answer is BOTH — FD for your emergency fund and short-term goals, equity mutual funds for long-term wealth building.

📱 Are apps like Groww, Zerodha safe for investing?▼

Yes — they are SEBI-regulated, your mutual fund units are held by NSDL/CDSL (government depositories), not by the app company. Even if Groww or Zerodha shut down tomorrow, your investments are safe and can be transferred to another broker. Always enable 2-factor authentication and never share your login OTP.

🏠 Is buying a house better than renting as a young professional?▼

In your 20s, renting is usually smarter — you maintain mobility (move for better jobs), avoid EMI burden in low-income years, and can invest the difference in equity for higher returns. Consider buying when: you plan to stay in one city for 10+ years, your EMI is below 40% of take-home pay, and you have a 20% down payment saved without emptying your emergency fund.

🔒 What to do if I get a job offer? First 5 financial steps?▼

1) Open a salary account and set up auto-SIP of ₹1,000–₹2,000/month in a Nifty 50 index fund from day one. 2) Get health insurance (employer's + a personal backup). 3) Get term insurance if you have dependents. 4) Start PPF contribution of ₹500/month. 5) File your first ITR by July 31 of the assessment year. These 5 steps in month 1 of your job will compound into crores by retirement.
